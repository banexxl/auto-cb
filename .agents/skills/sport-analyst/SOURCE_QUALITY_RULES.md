# Source Quality Rules

## Best Sources
Use these first:

- Official team/club/player websites
- Official league/federation announcements
- Official injury and disciplinary reports
- Press conference transcripts/videos
- Reputable sports outlets
- Recognized statistical providers
- Court, police, federation, or integrity-unit reports for match-fixing claims

## Medium-Trust Sources
Use carefully:

- Local sports media
- Beat reporters
- Verified journalists on social media
- Club-focused outlets
- Betting-market trackers

## Low-Trust Sources
Do not rely on these alone:

- Anonymous social media posts
- Fan forums
- Telegram/Discord rumors
- Unverified betting tips
- “Insider” accounts without a track record
- Clickbait sites

## Claim Labels
Every important claim should be labeled when uncertainty exists:

- Confirmed: official or multiple reliable sources agree.
- Reported: credible media reports it, but not official.
- Rumored: weak source or social media claim.
- Inferred: analyst conclusion from data/context.
- Unknown: no reliable information found.

## Match-Fixing and Integrity Claims
For integrity-related topics, require stronger evidence than normal sports news.

Acceptable evidence:

- Official sanctions
- Federation disciplinary records
- Court documents
- Police investigation statements
- Recognized betting-integrity alerts
- Reputable investigative reporting

Not enough by itself:

- Odds movement
- Bad performance
- Strange lineup
- Fan suspicion
- Social media claims
- One-sided scoreline

Always separate “risk signal” from “proof.”
